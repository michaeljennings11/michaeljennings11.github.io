Thermal Instability

Thermal instability (TI) refers to a runaway cooling/heating process in a plasma that results in condensation of cold gas clouds forming within a hotter background medium. TI criteria were first analyzed in Field (1965) and have since been studied in detail through numerical simulations. TI is used to explain the origins of the multiphase structure in the Interstellar Medium (ISM), as well as the existence of cooler gas clouds in the Intracluster Medium (ICM) and Circumgalactic Medium (CGM).

Below are simulations of TI within the ISM. I use the magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) code Athena++ to simulate the formation of cold clouds from uniform background. The first movie shows TI for pure hydrodynamic simulations without thermal conduction for various spectral index alpha. The next simulations include thermal conduction and viscosity with Pr = 10-2 (low viscosity) and Pr = 2/3 (high viscosity). The final set of simulations include a magnetic field initially oriented 45 degrees above the horizontal axis. The first movie has initial plasma beta of 106 and the second has initial plasma beta of 1.

Gravitational Instability / Gravito-Turbulence

The cold, outer portions of thin circumstellar disks where radiative cooling is relatively efficient have been shown to be gravitationally unstable. Numerical simulations of the non-linear outcome of gravitational instability often result in fragmentation of the disk to form "planetesimals" when cooling is sufficiently rapid. For moderate cooling, the instability and resulting collapse causes shocks that heat the disk until cooling can occur again, setting up a self-sustaining gravito-turbulence.

Below is a 3D simulation of a self-gravitating gravito-turbulent disk seeded by the initial gravitational instability. The Athena simulation utilizes the local shearing box approximation where the reference frame is rotating with a box centered on a constant radius such that the local angular frequency can be considered constant. The disk is initially in hydrostatic equilibrium and is then seeded with random small amplitude velocity fluctuations. The cooling time is tuned down until the disk becomes susceptible to fragmentation. Two fragments form toward the end and merge.
